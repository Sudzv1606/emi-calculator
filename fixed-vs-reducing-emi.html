Fixed vs. Floating Rate EMI

Published on February 26, 2025

For borrowers in the US and Canada, the choice between a fixed-rate and floating-rate Equated Monthly Installment (EMI) significantly impacts loan repayment stability, costs, and budgeting. A fixed-rate EMI locks in an interest rate for the entire loan term, ensuring consistent payments, while a floating-rate EMI adjusts based on market conditions, potentially increasing or decreasing payments. This guide explores these options, their implications for home loans in Toronto, auto loans in New York, and more, using our free EMI calculator for precise planning.

The distinction between fixed and floating rates has evolved with North American lending practices. In the US, fixed-rate mortgages have dominated since the 1930s, driven by FHA programs, with 90% of new mortgages fixed-rate as of 2024, per Freddie Mac. In Canada, the CMHC (Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation) reports that 85% of new mortgages in 2025 are fixed-rate, averaging 5.7%, while floating rates, tied to the Canadian Prime Rate, account for 15%. How Fixed and Floating Rate EMIs Work

Fixed Rate EMI: The interest rate remains constant throughout the loan term, ensuring your EMI stays predictable. This suits borrowers seeking stability, especially in rising rate environments. For example, a $200,000 US home loan at a fixed 6.5% over 30 years yields an EMI of approximately $1,264.14, as calculated with the formula:

EMI = P × r × (1 + r)^n / [(1 + r)^n - 1]

Where P = $200,000, r = 0.065 ÷ 12 ≈ 0.005417, and n = 360 months. Total interest paid is approximately $155,093.04, and total repayment is $355,093.04.

Floating Rate EMI: The rate adjusts periodically based on benchmarks like the US Federal Funds Rate or Canadian Prime Rate, potentially altering your EMI or tenure. A $200,000 US loan starting at 6.5%, rising to 7.5% after 5 years, might see EMI increase to $1,398.43, adding $18,339.76 in interest, totaling $373,432.80 paid.

In Canada, a CAD 250,000 mortgage at a fixed 5.7% over 25 years has an EMI of CAD 1,548.57, with total interest of CAD 114,571.00. A floating rate starting at 5.7%, rising to 6.7% after 5 years, could increase EMI to CAD 1,720.45, adding CAD 11,564.00 in interest, totaling CAD 376,135.00.

Benefits and Risks of Fixed vs. Floating Rates

Fixed Rate Benefits: Predictable payments shield borrowers from rate hikes, ideal for long-term planning in volatile markets, like California or Ontario. A 2024 US Mortgage Bankers Association study found fixed-rate borrowers saved an average of $15,000 over 30 years versus floating-rate borrowers in rising rates.

Floating Rate Benefits: Potential savings if rates fall, as seen in Canada’s 2008–2010 recession, where floating-rate borrowers saved 12% on interest, per FCAC data. However, risks include higher costs if rates rise, as 20% of Canadian borrowers faced in 2022–2023, per Bank of Canada reports.

Common misconceptions include assuming fixed rates are always costlier—however, they protect against market volatility, while floating rates suit risk-takers betting on rate drops, like in Seattle or Quebec.
